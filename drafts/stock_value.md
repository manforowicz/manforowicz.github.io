What makes a company's stock price rise when a company makes profits?

I think I finally have a good answer.
But first, let's look at common unsatisfying answers.


## Unsatisfying answer 1: "ownership"

> A share (also known as a stock) represents ownership of a piece of the company.
> For example, if you own 50% of a company's shares, you own 50% of the company.
> When the company makes a profit, more people want to own it,
> so its price rises.

Sure, if I own a physical lemonade stand, I can sell lemonade and make money.
But a share just feels like a piece of paper with the company's logo on it. I don't feel like an owner of that company.
Why should its profits affect the value of my piece of paper?

## Unsatisfying answer 2: "self-fulfilling prophecy"

> Pieces of paper can have a price.
> For example, Dogecoin is a useless "piece of paper" with a price.
> When people believe its price will rise, they buy, hoping to sell later.
> But the wave of people buying _is_ what makes the price rise (by increasing demand and reducing supply).
> This forms a self-fulfilling prophecy: people believe the price of Dogecoin will rise, so they all buy it, which makes it rise.
>
> This is one mechanism that links share price to company performance.
> The company does well, so people belive its share price will rise, so they all buy it, which causes the share price to rise.

Sure, I believe self-fulfilling prophecies do influcence a share's price, but I doubt that's the only mechanism at play.

If shares are just pieces of paper, why are all company executives so obsessed about their price?
Why don't companies go about their business, completely ignoring the existence of their shares?

## Analysis

The first question we should ask is what do shares have that pieces of paper don't? These things:

- **Dividends**. When a company decides on a dividend of $10, that means it will pay shareholders $10 per year for each share they hold. So if you hold 15 shares, the companitsy will pay you $150 a year.

- **Share buybacks**. A company can decide to buy its own shares back from the market, making them essentially disappear.

- **IPOs (initial public offerings)**. A company can decide to create new shares out of thin air and sell them.

- **Voting rights**. For each share you own, you get one vote on shareholder matters. You can elect board members. Board members are the people who control when the company does dividends, share buybacks, and IPOs. Board members also hire executives to run the company for them.

Sure, how do these things affect share price?

## My understanding

Imagine you own 51% of a company's shares.
As a greedy human, you want to use this fact to make money, but how?
Well, you have 51% of the vote, so you can control the board of directors by electing members that will do what you want.

You could tell the board members to give you a high salary using  company revenue.
But high salaries are heavily taxed.
Is there a more tax evasive way to get money in your pocket?

Yes! You can tell the board members to spend company revenue on dividends, which aren't taxed as heavily.
Since you own 51% of the shares, you'll get 51% of the dividends.

You could also tell board members to spend company revenue on share buybacks, which are taxed even less. These increase demand and decrease supply, causing share price to rise, benefiting you as the majority shareholder.

The more profits the company generates, the more it can spend on dividends and buybacks, which causes people to want to buy its shares.
And that is why share price rises when a company does well.

## Question 1

> Dividends and buybacks are leaky, meaning they give some money to non-majority shareholders. Are majority shareholders annoyed at this?

Quite the opposite. When a company does well and spends money on dividends or buybacks, more people want to buy shares, which causes their price to rise even further! This benefits the majority shareholder, in addition to the dividends or buybacks themselves.

My hypothesis is that the non-majority shareholders act like amplifiers of dividends and buybacks, which offsets their leakiness. But I'm not sure to what degree. 


## Question 2

> But as of 2025, Amazon has never paid a dividend, and didn't do significant buybacks. So why do people still hold its shares?

Imagine again you're a majority shareholder. You can tell the board to not give dividends and buybacks, and instead reinvest profits into expanding business.
This increases future profits, allowing even larger future dividends and buybacks.

Ordinary people realize this, so they buy shares, causing their price to rise.
So dividends and buybacks aren't necessary to raise share price. The mere expectation of them is enough!
